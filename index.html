What is Spinocerebellar Ataxia?

Spinocerebellar ataxia (SCA) is a group of hereditary neurodegenerative diseases caused by the progressive degeneration of the cerebellum ‚Äî the part of the brain that controls movement and coordination. There are more than 40 known genetic subtypes, and together they affect an estimated 150,000 Americans.

"Because the brain cannot regenerate, every day without treatment means irreversible loss ‚Äî of movement, independence, and time."

Early symptoms include difficulty walking, impaired balance, slurred speech, and problems with fine motor tasks such as writing or swallowing. Over time, patients lose the ability to walk independently and often require a wheelchair. Many subtypes also affect vision, cognition, and the ability to speak or swallow safely.

SCA is relentlessly progressive. There is currently no FDA-approved therapy and no cure for any subtype. Most patients experience a steady decline over 10‚Äì25 years, with significantly reduced life expectancy depending on the subtype. SCA Type 3 (SCA3), the most common form worldwide, is typically fatal, with patients often dying in their 50s or 60s from complications such as aspiration pneumonia.

Because the brain does not regenerate lost neurons, every delay in treatment translates directly into permanent, unrecoverable decline. This is why regulatory flexibility ‚Äî and drugs like Troriluzole ‚Äî matter so urgently for patients like Erin.
